Human Anatomy

A complete student guide — from the foundations of anatomy through the end of Splanchnology. Written for clarity, designed for retention.

Chapter 1 Introduction to Anatomy

What Is Anatomy?

Anatomy (Greek: anatomē = "cutting up") is the science of body structure and the relationships between structures. It is one of the oldest medical sciences, dating back to Hippocrates and Galen.

Anatomy is studied alongside Physiology — the science of body functions. Structure always determines function.

Anatomical Position & Terminology

All descriptions in anatomy assume the standard anatomical position: the body stands erect, face forward, arms at sides with palms facing forward, feet together.

Body Planes

Sagittal plane — divides body into left and right. Frontal (coronal) plane — divides into anterior and posterior. Transverse (horizontal) plane — divides into superior and inferior.

Body Cavities

The body has two main cavities: the dorsal cavity (cranial + vertebral) and the ventral cavity (thoracic + abdominopelvic). The thoracic cavity contains the lungs (pleural cavities) and the heart (pericardial cavity), separated from the abdominopelvic cavity by the diaphragm.

Chapter 2 Cells & Tissues

The Cell — Basic Unit of Life

Every human body is composed of approximately 37 trillion cells. Each cell has a plasma membrane, cytoplasm (with organelles), and a nucleus containing DNA.

Key Organelles

OrganelleFunction
NucleusContains DNA; controls cell activity
MitochondriaATP production — "powerhouse of the cell"
Rough ERProtein synthesis (has ribosomes)
Smooth ERLipid synthesis; detoxification
Golgi apparatusPackages & ships proteins
LysosomesDigest waste — "recycling center"
RibosomesTranslate mRNA into proteins

The Four Primary Tissue Types

1. Epithelial Tissue

Covers body surfaces, lines cavities, and forms glands. It is avascular (no blood vessels) and has a high regeneration rate. Classification is by layers (simple vs. stratified) and cell shape (squamous, cuboidal, columnar).

2. Connective Tissue

The most abundant tissue. It supports, protects, and binds organs together. Examples include loose connective tissue, dense connective tissue, cartilage, bone, and blood. All connective tissues have cells embedded in an extracellular matrix.

3. Muscle Tissue

Responsible for movement. Three types: skeletal (voluntary, striated), cardiac (involuntary, striated, intercalated discs), and smooth (involuntary, non-striated, found in organ walls).

4. Nervous Tissue

Found in the brain, spinal cord, and nerves. Neurons transmit electrical signals; neuroglia (glial cells) support and protect neurons.

🔑 Key Concept: Every organ is composed of at least two (usually all four) tissue types working together. The study of tissues is called histology.

Chapter 3 Osteology — The Skeletal System

Overview

The adult skeleton has 206 bones divided into the axial skeleton (80 bones — skull, vertebral column, thoracic cage) and the appendicular skeleton (126 bones — limbs and girdles).

Functions of Bone

Support, protection (skull protects the brain, ribs protect the heart/lungs), movement (levers for muscles), mineral storage (calcium, phosphorus), blood cell formation (hematopoiesis in red marrow), and fat storage (yellow marrow).

Bone Classification by Shape

Bone Structure

A long bone has a diaphysis (shaft) made of compact bone surrounding a medullary cavity, and two epiphyses (ends) made mostly of spongy (cancellous) bone covered by a thin shell of compact bone. The periosteum is a fibrous membrane covering the outer surface. The endosteum lines the inner surface.

The epiphyseal plate (growth plate) between the diaphysis and epiphysis is where longitudinal bone growth occurs in children. In adults it becomes the epiphyseal line.

The Skull (22 bones)

The cranium (8 bones): frontal, 2 parietal, occipital, 2 temporal, sphenoid, ethmoid. The face (14 bones): 2 maxillae, mandible, 2 zygomatic, 2 nasal, 2 lacrimal, 2 palatine, 2 inferior nasal conchae, vomer.

The Vertebral Column (26 bones)

7 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 1 sacrum (5 fused), 1 coccyx (3–5 fused). The vertebral column has four curvatures: cervical lordosis, thoracic kyphosis, lumbar lordosis, and sacral kyphosis.

The Thoracic Cage

12 pairs of ribs (7 true, 3 false, 2 floating), the sternum (manubrium, body, xiphoid process), and 12 thoracic vertebrae.

Upper Limb Bones

Clavicle, scapula, humerus, radius, ulna, 8 carpals, 5 metacarpals, 14 phalanges.

Lower Limb Bones

Hip bone (ilium, ischium, pubis), femur, patella, tibia, fibula, 7 tarsals, 5 metatarsals, 14 phalanges.

💡 Memory Aid: To remember the 8 carpal bones: "Some Lovers Try Positions That They Can't Handle" — Scaphoid, Lunate, Triquetrum, Pisiform, Trapezium, Trapezoid, Capitate, Hamate.

Chapter 5 Myology — The Muscular System

Types of Muscle Tissue

Skeletal muscle — voluntary, striated, attached to bones. Cardiac muscle — involuntary, striated, only in the heart. Smooth muscle — involuntary, non-striated, in organ walls and vessels.

Skeletal Muscle Structure

Each skeletal muscle is an organ. From outer to inner: epimysium (wraps entire muscle) → perimysium (wraps fascicles) → endomysium (wraps individual fibers). Inside each fiber are myofibrils made of sarcomeres — the functional contractile units.

How Muscles Work

Muscles have an origin (fixed attachment) and an insertion (movable attachment). When a muscle contracts, it pulls the insertion toward the origin. Muscles work in groups: the agonist (prime mover) performs the action; the antagonist opposes it; synergists assist; and fixators stabilize the origin.

Major Muscle Groups

Head & Neck

Muscles of facial expression (orbicularis oculi, orbicularis oris, buccinator, frontalis, zygomaticus), muscles of mastication (masseter, temporalis, medial & lateral pterygoids), sternocleidomastoid (head rotation), and the suprahyoid and infrahyoid groups.

Trunk

Anterior abdominal wall: external oblique, internal oblique, transversus abdominis, rectus abdominis (the "six-pack"). Back: erector spinae group (iliocostalis, longissimus, spinalis), trapezius, latissimus dorsi. Thorax: external & internal intercostals, diaphragm (the most important muscle of respiration).

Upper Limb

Shoulder: deltoid, rotator cuff (supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis — "SITS"). Arm: biceps brachii (flexion), triceps brachii (extension). Forearm: flexor and extensor groups. Hand: thenar, hypothenar, lumbricals, interossei.

Lower Limb

Hip: gluteus maximus, medius, minimus, iliopsoas. Thigh: quadriceps femoris (extension), hamstrings (flexion), adductor group. Leg: tibialis anterior (dorsiflexion), gastrocnemius & soleus (plantarflexion). Foot: intrinsic muscles.

💡 Rotator Cuff Mnemonic — "SITS": Supraspinatus, Infraspinatus, Teres minor, Subscapularis.

Chapter 6 Angiology — The Cardiovascular & Lymphatic Systems

The Heart

A muscular pump located in the mediastinum, slightly left of the midline. It has 4 chambers: right atrium, right ventricle, left atrium, left ventricle. Deoxygenated blood enters the right atrium → right ventricle → pulmonary trunk → lungs. Oxygenated blood returns via pulmonary veins → left atrium → left ventricle → aorta → body.

Valves prevent backflow: tricuspid (right AV), bicuspid/mitral (left AV), pulmonary semilunar, and aortic semilunar.

The heart wall has three layers: endocardium (inner), myocardium (muscular middle), and epicardium (outer, visceral pericardium).

Blood Vessels

ArteriolesThin muscular wallRegulate blood flow to capillaries

Major Arteries

Aorta → ascending aorta → aortic arch (brachiocephalic trunk, left common carotid, left subclavian) → descending aorta → thoracic aorta → abdominal aorta → common iliac arteries → external & internal iliac. The celiac trunk, superior & inferior mesenteric arteries supply abdominal organs.

Lymphatic System

Consists of lymphatic vessels, lymph nodes, and lymphoid organs (spleen, thymus, tonsils). It drains excess interstitial fluid, transports dietary fats, and provides immune defense. The thoracic duct is the largest lymphatic vessel, draining most of the body into the left subclavian vein.

⚠️ Clinical Note: Coronary artery disease (blockage of the coronary arteries supplying the myocardium) is the leading cause of death worldwide. The left anterior descending artery is called the "widow-maker" because its blockage is often fatal.

Chapter 7 The Nervous System

Divisions

Central Nervous System (CNS): brain + spinal cord. Peripheral Nervous System (PNS): cranial nerves (12 pairs) + spinal nerves (31 pairs). The PNS is further divided into a somatic (voluntary) division and an autonomic (involuntary) division. The autonomic nervous system has sympathetic ("fight or flight") and parasympathetic ("rest and digest") branches.

The Neuron

The functional unit. It has a cell body (soma), dendrites (receive signals), and an axon (transmits signals). Many axons are wrapped in myelin sheath to speed conduction. Signals pass between neurons at synapses via neurotransmitters.

The Brain

Spinal Cord & Spinal Nerves

The spinal cord extends from the foramen magnum to approximately L1–L2 (conus medullaris). Below that, spinal nerve roots form the cauda equina. There are 31 pairs of spinal nerves: 8 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral, 1 coccygeal.

Chapter 8 Sense Organs (Esthesiology)

The Eye (Organ of Vision)

The eyeball sits in the bony orbit. It has three layers: fibrous tunic (sclera + cornea), vascular tunic/uvea (choroid, ciliary body, iris), and retina (photoreceptors — rods for dim light, cones for color). Light passes through the cornea → aqueous humor → pupil → lens → vitreous humor → retina. The optic nerve (CN II) carries signals to the brain.

The Ear (Organ of Hearing & Balance)

Three parts: External ear (auricle + ear canal + tympanic membrane), Middle ear (3 ossicles: malleus, incus, stapes — connected to inner ear via oval window), Inner ear (cochlea for hearing; semicircular canals + vestibule for balance). The vestibulocochlear nerve (CN VIII) transmits signals.

Taste & Smell

Taste buds are mainly on the tongue (papillae), detecting sweet, sour, salty, bitter, and umami. Olfactory receptors are in the nasal cavity roof, with axons passing through the cribriform plate to the olfactory bulb (CN I).

Chapter 9 Introduction to Splanchnology

What Is Splanchnology?

Splanchnology (Greek: splanchnon = viscus) is the branch of anatomy dealing with the visceral organs — organs located primarily within the thoracic, abdominal, and pelvic cavities. It encompasses five major organ systems:

  1. Digestive (Alimentary) System
  2. Respiratory System
  3. Urinary System
  4. Reproductive (Genital) System
  5. Endocrine Glands

General Principles of Visceral Organs

Visceral organs are typically hollow (tubular organs with a lumen — e.g., stomach, intestines) or parenchymatous (solid organs — e.g., liver, kidneys). Hollow organs generally have a four-layered wall:

Layer (inside → out)Description
Tunica mucosaEpithelium + lamina propria + muscularis mucosae. Lines the lumen. Contains glands.
Tela submucosaLoose connective tissue with blood vessels, nerves (submucosal plexus of Meissner), glands in some organs.
Tunica muscularisUsually two layers of smooth muscle: inner circular + outer longitudinal. Contains myenteric plexus of Auerbach between the layers.
Tunica adventitia / serosaAdventitia = connective tissue (retroperitoneal organs). Serosa = connective tissue + mesothelium (intraperitoneal organs).

Topographic Description of Organs

Each organ is described by three relationships:

  • Holotopy — Location in body regions (e.g., "epigastrium")
  • Skeletotopy — Projection onto the skeleton (e.g., "T11–L2")
  • Syntopy — Relationship to neighboring organs
🔑 Big Picture: Splanchnology ties together everything you've learned. The nervous system innervates viscera; the blood vessels supply them; muscles and bones form the cavities that house them. Everything connects.

Chapter 10 The Digestive (Alimentary) System

The digestive system extends from the lips to the anus (~9 m total) and includes associated glands. Its function: ingestion, mechanical & chemical digestion, absorption, and defecation.

1. Oral Cavity (Mouth)

Divided into the vestibule (between lips/cheeks and teeth) and the oral cavity proper (bounded by the teeth, palate, and floor of the mouth).

Hard & Soft Palate

The hard palate forms the bony roof (maxillae + palatine bones). The soft palate is muscular and ends in the uvula. The palatoglossal and palatopharyngeal arches flank the palatine tonsils in the tonsillar fossa.

The Tongue

A muscular organ with a root, body, and apex. The dorsal surface has papillae containing taste buds: filiform (most numerous, no taste buds), fungiform, vallate (arranged in a V before the terminal sulcus), and foliate. The lingual tonsil lies posterior to the terminal sulcus.

Teeth

Children: 20 deciduous teeth. Adults: 32 permanent teeth. Each tooth has a crown (enamel), neck, and root (cementum). Inside: dentin surrounds the pulp cavity. The dental formula for permanent teeth is 2-1-2-3 per quadrant (incisors, canine, premolars, molars).

Salivary Glands

Three pairs of major glands: parotid (serous, duct opens near 2nd upper molar), submandibular (mixed, duct opens at sublingual caruncle), sublingual (mucous, multiple ducts). Saliva contains water, mucus, and amylase for starch digestion.

2. Pharynx

A 12–14 cm musculomembranous tube behind the nasal, oral, and laryngeal cavities. Three parts: nasopharynx (pharyngeal tonsil + auditory tube openings), oropharynx (palatine tonsils; food & air cross here), and laryngopharynx (piriform recesses; continues into esophagus). The tonsils form Waldeyer's (Pirogov's) lymphoid ring.

Muscles: three constrictors (superior, middle, inferior) + three longitudinal (stylopharyngeus, salpingopharyngeus, palatopharyngeus).

3. Esophagus

A 23–25 cm muscular tube from pharynx (C6) to stomach (T11). Three parts: cervical, thoracic, abdominal. Three anatomical constrictions: pharyngeal (at origin), bronchial/aortic (at tracheal bifurcation), and diaphragmatic (at esophageal hiatus). The wall has four layers; there is no serosa — only adventitia.

The upper ⅓ has skeletal muscle, middle ⅓ is mixed, and lower ⅓ is smooth muscle.

4. Stomach

The most dilated portion of the GI tract. Located in the epigastrium and left hypochondrium (T11–L1/L2). It has a cardiac part (entrance), fundus (dome), body, and pyloric part (antrum + canal). Two curvatures: lesser (right, attached to lesser omentum) and greater (left, attached to greater omentum). Two sphincters: cardiac and pyloric.

The wall has four layers. The muscular coat has three layers (longitudinal, circular, oblique). The mucosa has gastric pits leading to gastric glands that secrete HCl, pepsinogen, mucus, and intrinsic factor.

5. Small Intestine (~6–7 m)

Duodenum (25 cm)

C-shaped, mostly retroperitoneal. Four parts: superior, descending, horizontal (inferior), ascending. The descending part receives the common bile duct and pancreatic duct at the major duodenal papilla (of Vater). The duodenojejunal flexure is held by the ligament of Treitz.

Jejunum & Ileum

The jejunum (upper ⅖) has thicker walls, more circular folds (plicae circulares), and a richer blood supply. The ileum (lower ⅗) has Peyer's patches (aggregated lymphoid follicles) and ends at the ileocecal valve. Both are intraperitoneal, suspended by the mesentery.

The mucosa has millions of villi (finger-like projections containing capillaries and a lacteal for fat absorption) and crypts of Lieberkühn (intestinal glands).

6. Large Intestine (~1.5 m)

Begins at the cecum and ends at the anus. Three distinguishing features: taeniae coli (three longitudinal muscle bands), haustra (pouches), and epiploic appendages (fat-filled peritoneal tags).

Parts: cecum (with appendix) → ascending colon → right colic (hepatic) flexure → transverse colon → left colic (splenic) flexure → descending colon → sigmoid colon → rectum → anal canal → anus.

The rectum has transverse rectal folds and ends in the anal canal with anal columns, anal valves, and two sphincters: internal (smooth muscle, involuntary) and external (skeletal muscle, voluntary).

7. Liver (Hepar)

The largest gland (~1.5 kg). Located in the right hypochondrium and epigastrium. Two surfaces: diaphragmatic (smooth) and visceral (with impressions). The porta hepatis on the visceral surface transmits the portal vein, hepatic artery, and common hepatic duct. Four lobes: right, left, caudate, quadrate. The functional unit is the hepatic lobule, with a central vein and peripheral portal triads.

Ligaments: falciform, coronary, right & left triangular, round ligament (ligamentum teres — remnant of umbilical vein), ligamentum venosum.

8. Gallbladder

A pear-shaped reservoir (30–50 mL) on the visceral surface of the liver. Parts: fundus, body, infundibulum, neck. The neck continues as the cystic duct, which joins the common hepatic duct to form the common bile duct (ductus choledochus). The common bile duct opens with the pancreatic duct at the major duodenal papilla, controlled by the sphincter of Oddi.

9. Pancreas

A retroperitoneal organ (~15 cm). Parts: head (in the duodenal curve), uncinate process, neck, body, tail (reaches the spleen). The main pancreatic duct (of Wirsung) opens at the major duodenal papilla; the accessory duct (of Santorini) opens at the minor papilla. The pancreas is both exocrine (digestive enzymes) and endocrine (islets of Langerhans — insulin & glucagon).

⚠️ Clinical Note: Appendicitis is inflammation of the appendix. McBurney's point (⅓ of the way from the right anterior superior iliac spine to the umbilicus) is the classic point of maximum tenderness.

Chapter 11 The Respiratory System

Function: supplies O₂ to blood and removes CO₂. Divided into the upper respiratory tract (nose, pharynx, larynx) and lower respiratory tract (trachea, bronchi, lungs).

1. Nose & Nasal Cavity

The external nose is formed by nasal bones and cartilages. The nasal cavity is divided by the nasal septum (perpendicular plate of ethmoid + vomer + septal cartilage). Each lateral wall has three conchae (superior, middle, inferior) creating three meatuses that warm, moisten, and filter air. The paranasal sinuses (maxillary, frontal, ethmoid, sphenoid) drain into the meatuses.

2. Larynx (Voice Box)

Located at C3–C6. Framework of cartilages: thyroid (largest, "Adam's apple"), cricoid (complete ring), epiglottis (elastic, covers glottis during swallowing), and paired arytenoid, corniculate, and cuneiform cartilages. The vocal folds (true vocal cords) stretch between the thyroid and arytenoid cartilages; the space between them is the rima glottidis.

3. Trachea (Windpipe)

A 10–12 cm tube from C6 to T4–T5, supported by 16–20 C-shaped hyaline cartilage rings (open posteriorly — trachealis muscle). At the carina (T4–T5) it bifurcates into right and left main (primary) bronchi. The right main bronchus is wider, shorter, and more vertical — foreign objects are more likely to enter it.

4. Bronchial Tree

Main bronchi → lobar (secondary) bronchi (3 right, 2 left) → segmental (tertiary) bronchi (10 per lung) → further divisions → terminal bronchioles → respiratory bronchioles → alveolar ducts → alveolar sacs → alveoli (~300 million per lung). Gas exchange occurs at the alveoli through the extremely thin respiratory membrane.

5. Lungs

Right lung: 3 lobes (superior, middle, inferior) separated by oblique and horizontal fissures, 10 bronchopulmonary segments. Left lung: 2 lobes (superior, inferior) separated by oblique fissure, 8–10 segments, plus a cardiac notch and lingula. Each lung has an apex (above clavicle), base (on diaphragm), costal surface, and mediastinal surface with the hilum (where bronchi, vessels, and nerves enter/exit, forming the lung root).

6. Pleura

A double-layered serous membrane: visceral pleura (covers lung surface) and parietal pleura (lines chest wall). Between them is the pleural cavity containing a thin film of serous fluid that reduces friction during breathing. Negative pressure in the pleural cavity keeps the lungs expanded.

💡 Remember: Right main bronchus = wider, shorter, more vertical → foreign bodies most commonly go RIGHT. Also: Right lung = 3 lobes, Left lung = 2 lobes (the heart takes the space).

Chapter 12 The Urinary System

Function: filters blood, produces urine, maintains water/electrolyte balance, regulates blood pressure (renin), and produces erythropoietin.

1. Kidneys

Paired, bean-shaped organs (~11 × 6 × 3 cm, ~150 g each), retroperitoneal at T12–L3 (right slightly lower due to the liver). Each kidney has a fibrous capsule, perirenal fat, renal fascia, and pararenal fat.

Internally: cortex (outer, contains glomeruli) → medulla (inner, contains renal pyramids whose tips are the renal papillae) → minor calyces → major calyces → renal pelvis → ureter. The hilum (medial border) transmits the renal artery, renal vein, and ureter.

The functional unit is the nephron (~1 million per kidney): renal corpuscle (glomerulus + Bowman's capsule) → proximal convoluted tubule → loop of Henle → distal convoluted tubule → collecting duct. Filtration occurs at the glomerulus; reabsorption and secretion occur along the tubule.

2. Ureters

Two muscular tubes (~25–30 cm) carrying urine from the renal pelvis to the urinary bladder by peristalsis. Each ureter has three constrictions: at the pelviureteric junction, at the crossing of the iliac vessels, and at the vesicoureteric junction (intramural part).

3. Urinary Bladder

A hollow muscular organ (capacity ~500 mL). Located behind the pubic symphysis. Its wall has the detrusor muscle (three layers of smooth muscle). The trigone is the smooth triangular area between the two ureteric orifices and the internal urethral orifice.

4. Urethra

Carries urine from the bladder to the exterior. In females: ~4 cm, opens anterior to the vaginal orifice. In males: ~20 cm, three parts — prostatic, membranous, and spongy (penile). The internal urethral sphincter (smooth, involuntary) and external urethral sphincter (skeletal, voluntary) control micturition.

⚠️ Clinical Note: Kidney stones (renal calculi) most commonly get stuck at the three ureteral constrictions, causing severe colicky pain (renal colic).

Chapter 13 The Reproductive (Genital) System

Male Reproductive System

Testes

Paired oval organs in the scrotum. Each testis has ~250 lobules containing seminiferous tubules (spermatogenesis) and interstitial cells of Leydig (testosterone). Tubules → rete testis → efferent ductules → epididymis → ductus (vas) deferens.

Spermatic Cord & Ductus Deferens

The spermatic cord contains the vas deferens, testicular artery, pampiniform venous plexus, nerves, and lymphatics. The vas deferens travels through the inguinal canal, crosses the ureter, and its terminal dilated part (ampulla) joins the duct of the seminal vesicle to form the ejaculatory duct, which opens into the prostatic urethra.

Accessory Glands

Seminal vesicles (fructose-rich alkaline fluid), prostate gland (surrounds the prostatic urethra; secretes slightly acidic fluid), bulbourethral glands (of Cowper; pre-ejaculatory mucus).

Penis

Contains three cylindrical bodies of erectile tissue: two corpora cavernosa (dorsal) and one corpus spongiosum (ventral, contains the spongy urethra, expands distally as the glans penis).

Female Reproductive System

Ovaries

Paired almond-shaped organs on the lateral pelvic wall, attached by the mesovarium, ovarian ligament, and suspensory ligament. They produce oocytes and hormones (estrogen, progesterone).

Uterine (Fallopian) Tubes

~10 cm long. Parts: infundibulum (with fimbriae), ampulla (usual site of fertilization), isthmus, uterine (intramural) part.

Uterus

A pear-shaped muscular organ (~7.5 × 5 × 2.5 cm). Parts: fundus, body, isthmus, cervix (supravaginal + vaginal parts). Wall layers: endometrium (mucosa — undergoes cyclic changes), myometrium (thick smooth muscle), perimetrium (serosa/peritoneum). Normal position: anteverted and anteflexed. Supported by broad, round, uterosacral, and cardinal (transverse cervical) ligaments.

Vagina

A fibromuscular canal (~8–10 cm) from the cervix to the vestibule. The cervix protrudes into the upper vagina, creating fornices (anterior, posterior, and two lateral). The posterior fornix is the deepest and is related to the rectouterine pouch (of Douglas).

External Genitalia (Vulva)

Mons pubis, labia majora, labia minora, clitoris, vestibule, vestibular (Bartholin's) glands, and vaginal orifice.

💡 Key relationship: The ureter passes UNDER the uterine artery ("water under the bridge") — an important surgical landmark.

Chapter 14 The Endocrine System

The endocrine system consists of ductless glands that secrete hormones directly into the bloodstream, regulating metabolism, growth, reproduction, and homeostasis.

Thyroid Gland — Anatomy

Two lateral lobes connected by an isthmus. Composed of follicles filled with colloid (thyroglobulin). Parafollicular (C) cells produce calcitonin. Blood supply: superior thyroid artery (from external carotid) and inferior thyroid artery (from thyrocervical trunk). The recurrent laryngeal nerve runs close to the thyroid — surgical importance.

Adrenal Glands — Anatomy

Right is triangular, left is semilunar. Each has an outer cortex (3 zones: zona glomerulosa → aldosterone; zona fasciculata → cortisol; zona reticularis → androgens) and an inner medulla (modified sympathetic ganglion → catecholamines).

💡 Adrenal cortex layers mnemonic — "GFR": Glomerulosa, Fasciculata, Reticularis (from outer to inner) → "Salt, Sugar, Sex" (aldosterone, cortisol, androgens).

Chapter 15 Peritoneum & Abdominal Topography

The Peritoneum

The largest serous membrane. Parietal peritoneum lines the abdominal wall. Visceral peritoneum covers organs. Between them is the peritoneal cavity (a potential space with ~50 mL of serous fluid). In males it is a closed sac; in females it communicates with the exterior through the uterine tubes.

Peritoneal Relationships of Organs

RelationshipDescriptionExamples
IntraperitonealAlmost completely covered by visceral peritoneumStomach, jejunum, ileum, transverse colon, sigmoid colon, spleen, liver
MesoperitonealCovered on three sidesAscending & descending colon, cecum (variable)
RetroperitonealBehind the peritoneum; covered only on frontKidneys, adrenals, aorta, IVC, pancreas (body/tail), duodenum (most), ureters

Peritoneal Folds & Structures

Mesentery — double layer of peritoneum suspending jejunum/ileum from posterior abdominal wall. Mesocolon — suspends the transverse and sigmoid colon. Lesser omentum — hepatogastric + hepatoduodenal ligaments (the latter contains the portal triad: portal vein, hepatic artery, bile duct). Greater omentum — "fatty apron" hanging from the greater curvature; acts as immune policeman.

Peritoneal Recesses

The omental bursa (lesser sac) communicates with the greater sac through the epiploic foramen (of Winslow). The rectouterine pouch (of Douglas) in females and the rectovesical pouch in males are the most dependent parts of the peritoneal cavity in the erect position — fluid collects here.

⚠️ Clinical Note: In peritonitis (infection of the peritoneal cavity), the greater omentum often migrates to the site of infection to wall it off — earning it the nickname "the abdominal policeman."

Review Self-Test Quizzes

Q1: How many bones does the adult human skeleton have?

✅ Correct! The adult skeleton has 206 bones: 80 axial + 126 appendicular.

Q2: Which layer of the digestive tract wall contains the myenteric (Auerbach's) plexus?

✅ Correct! Auerbach's plexus lies between the inner circular and outer longitudinal layers of the tunica muscularis. Meissner's plexus is in the submucosa.

Q3: The right main bronchus is more likely to receive aspirated foreign bodies because it is:

✅ Correct! The right main bronchus is wider, shorter, and more vertical than the left — a direct chute for foreign objects.

Q4: The functional unit of the kidney is the:

✅ Correct! Each kidney has about 1 million nephrons, each consisting of a renal corpuscle and a tubule system.

Q5: Which peritoneal fold is known as the "abdominal policeman"?

✅ Correct! The greater omentum migrates to sites of inflammation to wall off infection.

Q6: The three zones of the adrenal cortex from outermost to innermost are:

✅ Correct! GFR — "Salt, Sugar, Sex" → Aldosterone, Cortisol, Androgens.

Q7: Which cranial nerve provides parasympathetic innervation to most thoracic and abdominal viscera?

✅ Correct! The vagus nerve (CN X) has the widest distribution of any cranial nerve, innervating structures from the pharynx down to the splenic flexure of the colon.

Q8: Fertilization of the ovum most commonly occurs in which part of the uterine tube?

✅ Correct! The ampulla is the widest and longest part of the uterine tube, and the most common site of fertilization.
